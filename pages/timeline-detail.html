Detailed Timeline

A comprehensive day-by-day account of the Great Emu War, including specific events, strategic decisions, and their consequences.

October 1932 - The Crisis Builds

1

October 1, 1932

Emu Migration Intensifies: Large flocks of emus begin their seasonal migration, moving through the Campion district. Farmers report significant crop damage as approximately 20,000 emus move through wheat fields.

Impact: Estimated 75% crop loss in affected areas

15

October 15, 1932

Farmers' Petition: Ex-servicemen farmers in the Campion and Walgoolan districts petition the Minister of Agriculture for assistance. They request military intervention to cull the emu population.

Key Figure: James Mitchell, Premier of Western Australia, supports the petition

28

October 28, 1932

Government Approval: The federal government approves military assistance. Major G.P.W. Meredith of the Royal Australian Artillery is assigned to lead the operation with two soldiers and two Lewis machine guns.

Equipment: 10,000 rounds of ammunition allocated for the operation

November 1932 - The War Begins

2

November 2, 1932 - Day 1

Operation Begins: Major Meredith and his team arrive in Campion. First engagement occurs when they encounter a flock of about 50 emus. The emus immediately scatter when fired upon, demonstrating their tactical awareness.

Results: Only a few emus killed, machine gun jams due to dust

3

November 3, 1932 - Day 2

Tactical Adjustment: Meredith attempts to ambush emus at a local dam where they come to drink. However, the emus prove to be extremely wary and maintain scouts while the main flock drinks.

Emu Tactics: Demonstrated organized behavior with sentinel posting

4

November 4, 1932 - Day 3

The Famous Jam: During an encounter with a large flock, the machine gun jams at a critical moment. The emus, instead of fleeing, seem to taunt the soldiers before casually walking away.

Media Report: "The emus have won the first round" - Perth Daily News

8

November 8, 1932 - First Withdrawal

Tactical Retreat: After a week of limited success (only 12 emus confirmed killed), Major Meredith withdraws his forces. The operation is temporarily suspended due to poor results and equipment issues.

Ammunition Used: 2,500 rounds for 12 confirmed kills

12

November 12, 1932 - Public Pressure

Farmer Protests: Local farmers protest the withdrawal, claiming the emu problem is worsening. International media begins covering the story, often with amusement at Australia's "war" against birds.

International Coverage: British newspapers mock the "Great Emu War"

13

November 13, 1932 - Operation Resumed

Second Phase: Under continued pressure, the military operation resumes with new tactics. Attempts are made to drive emus into ambush positions, but the birds continue to demonstrate superior mobility.

New Strategy: Mobile hunting parties replace fixed positions

25

November 25, 1932 - Limited Success

Modest Gains: By the end of November, military forces report killing approximately 200 emus, but thousands remain. The cost in ammunition and man-hours is deemed excessive for the results achieved.

Efficiency: Approximately 40 rounds per confirmed emu kill

December 1932 - The Final Chapter

2

December 2, 1932

Final Push: Major Meredith makes one last concentrated effort, focusing on the largest remaining flocks. Despite improved tactics and positioning, the emus continue to outmaneuver the military forces.

Emu Adaptation: Birds now flee at the first sight of military vehicles

10

December 10, 1932 - Operation Ends

Official Withdrawal: The military operation is officially terminated. Major Meredith submits his final report, acknowledging the failure to significantly reduce the emu population despite extensive efforts.

Final Tally: ~986 emus killed, ~10,000 rounds fired, 3 soldiers deployed

15

December 15, 1932 - Aftermath

Alternative Solutions: The government announces a bounty system for emu culling, to be carried out by civilian hunters rather than military personnel. This proves more effective and cost-efficient.

New Approach: Civilian bounty hunters kill over 57,000 emus in 6 months

31

December 31, 1932 - Historical Legacy

Year's End Reflection: As 1932 ends, the Great Emu War becomes a subject of both embarrassment and amusement. The event demonstrates the challenges of using conventional military tactics against unconventional opponents.

Lesson Learned: Sometimes the most unusual problems require the most creative solutions
